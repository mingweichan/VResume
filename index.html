Selected Projects* UX focus


  • 1. Darlington Terrace

    concept, prototyping, testing, design

    This project was to design a terrace house in Darlington. The client was myself, and thus came the questions of how I wanted to represent myself and what my ideal dwelling place would be. I learnt that a dwelling must be easy to use, and fun! The concept model (next slide) was based upon the angles of the adjacent streets. It explored the ideas of fragmentation and conflict between two opposing facades. There was also an element of light capture from the North East direction which the light model in iteration 1 specifically addresses. A heliodome was used to gauge the lighting and shadow effects. Iteration 2 focused on the material components of the building and the quality/atmosphere of the various spaces.

  • 3. Lawn Bowl Bakery Bridge*

    concept, prototyping, interactive space

    This project was a journey of questioning, and creating a solution for a curve-ball brief. The task was to create a space that was a lawn bowls club, a bakery, and that would span between an expanse (aka a bridge).
    CONCEPT IDEA: To form a concept, I had to ask myself some interesting questions. What is a lawn bowls club, who goes there, what in essence is a bakery, should I explore the traditional bakery or the modern patisserie, what happens when these spaces conflict, what atmosphere results from the mixture of people who dwell in either etc. The model I created centres on the idea of balance; the balance of ingredients for a baker, and the physical balance that a player must possess in himself and the bowl.
    CONCEPT IDEA 2: This concept explores ideas of energy expense: the kinetic energy of lawn bowl playing and the chemical energy of baking.
    Iteration 2: The second iteration centred on the idea of letting the cafe bakery be a viewing area over the lawn. Thus the space became a stadium visually for the viewers of lawn bowls and an exhibition of the smell of the bakery.

  • 6. GoFar UX research

    user interview, usability testing, diary studies

    This work was done at a short three-week internship in a startup that is creating the future of behaviour-driven fuel efficiency. The company uses a mixture of hardware and software that tracks a car's sweetspot and suggests to the user methods of improving their driving in order to save more fuel.
    FIRST ITERATION WIREFRAMES: These were done by my colleaque, and there were some great ideas here. The idea of a weekly review would be useful, as well as being able to see your last trip, which would be most relevant to users. However, there were some problems.
    USABILITY INTERVIEWS: We conducted a lot of interviews with Fishburner residents, Kickstarter customers and people who were testing our product. From these raw notes, some obvious problems stood out to us. One of them was that the graph did not make sense without axes. Another was that the screen was quite cluttered.
    FEATURE SPECS: From there, I sorted the feedback into the respective feature folders, collating and organising so that it would be easier to see what trends there were amongst users for each feature. In this way, some subtler insights were gained. One of these insights were that users didn't just want a mess of statistics. In fact, some of them were put off by all the statistics, because they didn't want to be treated like a car geek. What they wanted were key statistics and relevant insights that could help them save fuel.

    SECOND ITERATION WIREFRAMES: Even though we weren't up to the interaction design stage, I decided to experiment with some colours to give the team an idea of what the release version might look like. These wireframes also included features which I thought would be most relevant to the user, based on customer feedback.

    For example, for the weekly statistics, I created a small carousel, so that the most relevant metric (acceleration and braking score) would be immediately obvious for the user. The user then has options to scroll through the carousel for deeper info, including a simple graph for their driving efficiency score throughout the week. Most users think they are very good drivers, so it is essential that they are shown their score to inform them that they are not driving to optimise fuel efficiency.

    Once the user knows they are not on a perfect score, a small chat box subtly gives relevant data-driven insights to help their behaviour. Inspired by Strava, a map with the route diagrammed instantly makes the last trip understandable, with further trips appearing below as if on a news feed.
